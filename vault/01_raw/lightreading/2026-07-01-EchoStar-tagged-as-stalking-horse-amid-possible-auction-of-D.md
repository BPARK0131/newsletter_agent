Politics Wireless News EchoStar tagged as stalking horse amid possible auction of Dish Wireless assets EchoStar has been chosen as the stalking horse bidder for the future sale of 'substantially all' of Dish Wireless's assets. A deadline of August 10 has been set to identify better bids for those assets, with an auction possibly to follow. Jeff Baumgartner , Senior Editor , Light Reading July 1, 2026 7 Min Read (Source: Kristoffer Tripplaar/Alamy Stock Photo) As part of Dish DBS&#x27;s Chapter 11 filings , the company intends to sell &quot;substantially all&quot; of Dish Wireless&#x27;s assets, which include tens of thousands of radios. Depending on which way the wind blows in the coming weeks, Dish parent EchoStar might end up paying for them. According to a First Day Declaration filed today (July 1), Dish Wireless parent company EchoStar is designated as the stalking horse bidder &quot;to any party that submits a bid determined to be higher or better than EchoStar&#x27;s proposal.&quot; A stalking horse bidder, which is chosen by the company filing bankruptcy, acts to set a minimum price floor so that other potential buyers cannot submit unreasonably low offers. Dish DBS&#x27;s First Day filing includes a lengthy declaration from John Swieringa, president, COO and board member of Dish Wireless, one of the debtors and debtors-in-possession in the voluntary and prepackaged Chapter 11 filing. A First Day hearing is scheduled for later today. Related: Dish turns to Chapter 11 Per the Stalking Horse Asset Purchase Agreement cited in the filing, the Dish Wireless debtors group kicked off a &quot;marketing process&quot; ahead of the Chapter 11 filings to identify any higher or better bids for the assets. The agreement established a bid deadline of August 10, 2026. &quot;If any qualifying bids are received by such deadline, an auction is proposed to be held on August 12, 2026, and the hearing to approve the sale(s) to the successful bidder(s) is proposed to proceed on August 17, 2026,&quot; the filing states. This approach &quot;sets up a third party, whether SpaceX or someone else to step in for the terrestrial network assets on the cheap, just as SpaceX assembles the spectrum that could use them,&quot; LightShed Partners analysts Walter Piecyk and Joe Galone explained in a research note (registration required). They view the &quot;cleanest path&quot; as SpaceX, Charter Communications or another third party buying the radios and then negotiating new leases with the tower companies. They also suspect that SpaceX might prefer to build a terrestrial network on its own, but acknowledge &quot;[i]t might be hard for SpaceX to pass up cheap radio assets with which it could negotiate favorable leases.&quot; As of May 5, 2026, Dish Wireless had deployed more than 144,000 radios across 24,000 US tower sites that, at one time, provided coverage to more than 80% of the country. The decommissioning process involves removing software from cell site equipment, turning down communications circuits, and deploying personnel to remove certain equipment from former 5G network sites or storing/disposing of equipment at various warehouses. Related: EchoStar could threaten bankruptcy over FCC inquiry Amid EchoStar&#x27;s spectrum sales last fall to AT&amp;T and SpaceX, which ended up remedying an FCC probe into the status of the company&#x27;s spectrum holdings and 5G network commitments, Dish Wireless decommissioned its 5G network while EchoStar&#x27;s Boost Mobile service transitioned to a hybrid MVNO model that runs on AT&amp;T&#x27;s mobile network. As an interesting aside, Swieringa noted in his declaration that it was &quot;unusual&quot; for the FCC to direct EchoStar to quickly ramp up the sale of spectrum or face possible license forfeitures. &quot;I understand that the FCC has never previously required a party to sell its spectrum licenses under threat of termination,&quot; the exec explained. Dish Wireless facing 170-plus lawsuits The decommissioning of the Dish Wireless network has led to a legal mess. Dish Wireless stopped paying tower companies and other infrastructure providers, claiming it was excused from those contracts because EchoStar&#x27;s spectrum sales made it impossible to operate its 5G network. Dish Wireless also contends that EchoStar, not Dish Wireless, owned that spectrum so therefore is not entitled to any proceeds from the sales.
