Politics News Japan commits $922M to Rakuten-AST satellite venture Japanese government will invest $922 million into Rakuten's joint venture with AST SpaceMobile amid Asian governments' anxiety over reliance on Starlink. Robert Clark , Contributing Editor , Light Reading July 1, 2026 2 Min Read (Source: Batchelder/Alamy Stock Photo) The Japanese government has committed 150 billion Japanese yen (US$922 million) to Rakuten Mobile&#x27;s low-Earth orbit (LEO) satellite JV with AST SpaceMobile. It will progressively invest in the project over the next three years to boost homegrown satellite communications and reduce reliance on Starlink. Japan&#x27;s Ministry of Internal Affairs and Communications secured the funds for the Japan Low Earth Orbit Satellite Communications Project (J-LEO) last year and then opened a tender process which concluded last month, the Japan Times reported . Japan is already one of the world’s most advanced D2D satellite markets. The three big operators — KDDI, NTT Docomo and Softbank – all provide services using Starlink, with KDDI reporting 4 million customers in its first year. But outside Japan, other countries in the region are expressing concern about being over-exposed to Starlink. Neighboring Taiwan is still grappling with whether Starlink should be involved at all in its domestic satellite market. Taiwan has been wary of the Elon Musk firm because of his strong business ties to China, which is one of Tesla’s biggest markets and a major manufacturing site. Sovereignty warnings Currently preventing Starlink from offering service in Taiwan is the 49% limit on foreign ownership of telecom service providers. Some legislators, wanting to accelerate the introduction of domestic satellite service, have proposed easing the ownership cap to allow Starlink to enter. However, Chen Chung-shu, acting head of the National Communications Commission, argued that Taiwan would need to install local gateways, as Japan had done, to ensure regulatory influence over satellite services. But he cautioned that the arrival of second generation Starlink, allowing multi-media satellite-to-satellite connectivity, would further reduce sovereignty. In Australia, two security agencies have issued warnings about being dependent on Starlink. The country was one of the first international markets for Starlink’s broadband service, which now has around 200,000 customers. But the Department of Homeland Affairs&#x27; cyber security unit recently warned of &quot;availability risks due to the offshore nature of Starlink,&quot; Australian Broadcasting Corp (ABC) has reported . The Australian Signals Directorate – the local equivalent to the US&#x27; NSA – had made a similar warning, cautioning that the influence of private satellite firms &quot;may exceed the regulatory capacity of individual nations, raising concerns about sovereignty and the protection of national interests.&quot; The Australian federal government has awarded more than a dozen contracts worth 7.2 million Australian dollars (US$4.9 million) to Starlink, including the Department of Defense and the electoral commission. Local and state agencies have issued a further A$40 million in contracts. Currently Starlink offers services all across east Asia with the exception of China and North Korea.
